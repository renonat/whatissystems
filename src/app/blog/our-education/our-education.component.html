Let's talk about what we learn

As a discipline, Systems Design Engineering (or SYDE for short) uses systems thinking and math to analyse and model the world, and design thinking to create solutions for users that move us towards a better future. The engineering problems that we tackle are commonly interdisciplinary, and require strong decision making and leadership skills.

We may pride ourselves on thinking differently, but we still are very much rooted in the physical world. SYDE is an accredited engineering program, and its graduates are eligible for the Professional Engineering designation through PEO. This means that we learn a lot of theory in math and physics, as well as applications in hardware and software. The first two years are primarily about accumulating knowledge about the real world through physics, and acquiring the hard analytic and math skills. This skill toolbox allows us to solve real, hands-on problems using systems theory.

Design is a complementary skill set that takes the form of proven processes for planning, creating, and executing designed solutions to problems. It is the perfect complement to engineering, and one that is rarely taught outright to engineers. Knowing human-centered design processes allows an engineer to think not just about the technical solution they are facilitating, but about all users and stakeholders who will interact with the solution, along with their individual requirements.

The skills that we learn in SYDE are inherently interdisciplinary, as they can be applied to almost any problem in a wide variety of fields. The program also has the flexibility to take many technical electives, so that students can specialize in their desired field of interest. design

I heard that it's human-centered?

Design thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation that draws from the designer’s toolkit to integrate the needs of people, the possibilities of technology, and the requirements for business success.

 Tim Brown, President and CEO of IDEO

What sets Systems apart from your typical engineering program is our emphasis on designing for everything, and everyone. Design is so much more than making things aesthetically pleasing. Design is a methodology to find and solve problems. Not only do we study and create software, but learn how to do so for people. We don’t stop at building bridges, but understand who will be using them and what their needs are. In short, the design process is a holistic and effective approach to solving technical issues while addressing human-centered ones that inevitably accompany them.

The design toolbox

Design is really an act of communication, which means having a deep understanding of the person with whom the designer is communicating.

 Tim Brown, President and CEO of IDEO

Our design courses cover a variety of tools that can be applied to any engineering problem. Our first design course (SYDE 161) demonstrates tools such as personas which are fictional characters used to help us solve problems from the perspective of a real user. We also learn how to use project management tools such as a Quality-Function-Deployment (QFD) chart which helps manage the project requirements and how the solution meets those requirements for each user and in comparison to competitors. SYDE 162 (Human Factors in Design) teaches us to consider the physical and mental limitations of humans while designing, so that we can increase usability and decrease errors. For example we use sets of principles, such as Norman’s Principles of Good Design, to guide evaluations of existing and future designs. Of great importance are also longer term design methods such as Google Ventures’ Sprint process, which allows a team to ideate and learn from users within the time span of a week.

systems

How we see the world? Like systems glasses?

If you were to ask each Systems Design professor to describe what Systems is, you would eventually hear the parable of the The Blind Men and the Elephant. The moral of the parable is that a system is greater than just the sum of its parts, and that you cannot know how a system will behave just by knowing the parts which make up the system. This is why you will hear the quote “Systems is how we see the world”, because systems is a way of thinking about the physical world which we interact with as engineers.

After hearing the parable of The Blind Men and the Elephant, see an example of systems thinking applied to a system familiar to everyone (5 min)

Systems thinking

Systems thinking tends to deal with complex problems, because the systems are interconnected sets of things —  like people, cells, or ecosystems — that produce changing behaviours over time due to their interactions. In fact, the entire world is one giant system, and we only focus on smaller sub-systems when looking at engineering problems. Our brains have a hard time wrapping themselves around systems, because we are built to interpret outside events as the causes of the change we see around us. The fact that systems cause their own behaviour because of their interconnectedness tends to break our mental models, and thus it takes a different way of thinking in order to rationally handle these large-scale problems.

Consider the following statement that a biomedical engineer may work with: “The flu virus does not attack you; you set up the conditions for it flourish within you.” If you find this statement counter-intuitive, you are not alone. We rationalize complex situations through our own experiences, and by following the patterns of cause and effect. By viewing problems through the lense of systems thinking, one can redesign a system to create lasting changes.

Modelling the world

As engineers, we apply our systems thinking to creating models of physical systems in order to understand engineering problems. This is why we take so many physics and math courses, so that we can apply our skills to modelling dynamic systems. Such problems may be as simple as a bathtub without a plug, being filled up at the same time that it is draining. Now throw in some salt and figure out the concentration of the water flowing from the bathtub and into the pipes. This easily translates to larger complex problems involving many moving parts, stresses, and applied forces.

Knowledge integration

While our systems modelling courses focus on the physical world, the skill of systems thinking can be easily translated to problems that touch both technology and the human environment. By attaching on the skill of design, one easily considers the underlying sociological, economic, political, and environmental systems that affect the problem space. This broad understanding of the world is where a Systems Design Engineer can really shine.
