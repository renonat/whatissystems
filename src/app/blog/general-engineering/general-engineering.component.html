People have trouble describing SYDE because it does not fit into a stereotype of what engineering work should be. But, this does not discredit the work that we do. While Systems Design may appear very broad, it is NOT a general engineering program that you might find at other Universities. We learn the specific skills of design and systems thinking. One than can apply these skills to their field of interest.

There is a trade-off that comes with being a SYDE student: deeply specializing in another field may need extracurricular work. For example, you will need to put in extra time to practice and learn to be as specialized as a mechanical engineer. Thus if you are highly passionate about a single field, consider directly pursuing that field. As a SYDE student, you must be proactive to find your niche. But, the knowledge you gain from SYDE will give you great freedom within your career.

Can I specialize?

While you can specialize through co-op terms and side projects, you can also take specialized electives. These electives allow you to become an expert at what YOU choose to focus on. There will be a great deal of freedom to learn in your field of choice, but it does need some thinking about disciplines beforehand. If you take a variety of diverse electives, there is a risk that you will end up as the “jack of all trades but master of none.” Give consideration to your elective choices, as they form the skillset that you take to the workforce as a practicing engineer.

If you do not know where your passions lie for your career, that is okay. Many spend their first two years exploring different fields through co-op positions. By midway through your degree, you should have an idea of your career goals and spend your time honing your skills. While it is okay to explore a variety of fields, be aware that it may impact the ease of finding co-op positions.
